History
Established in 2020 and based in Washington, DC,
[Democracy Docket](https://www.democracydocket.com/about-us/) is a progressive news source focused on voting rights and electoral court cases. [Marc Elias](https://www.elias.law/team/marc-elias), former [counsel](https://www.chambers-associate.com/the-big-interview/marc-elias-general-counsel-for-hillary-clinton) for Hillary Clinton’s 2016 and John Kerry’s 2004 campaigns, founded Elias Law Group in 2021. He previously led the political law practice at [Perkins Coie](https://www.linkedin.com/in/marceelias/) and represented major Democratic organizations and US Congressional leaders. [Read our profile on the United States government and media.](https://mediabiasfactcheck.com/united-states-media-profile/)
Funded by / Ownership
Democracy Docket funds its operations through
[donations](https://www.democracydocket.com/support/) with no specific mention of corporate, political, or other institutional funding sources on its [“About Us”](https://www.democracydocket.com/about-us/#:~:text=We%20depend%20on%20your%20support,unless%20we%20know%20what%E2%80%99s%20happening) page. However, the [Democracy Docket Legal Fund](https://democracyfund.org/grant/democracy-docket-legal-fund/#:~:text=Democracy%20Docket%20Legal%20Fund%2C%20a,laws%20and%20practices%20through%20litigation), also founded by Marc Elias, is a fiscally sponsored project of the Hopewell Fund, indicating additional support structures beyond public donations.
Analysis / Bias
Democracy Docket, known for its focus on voting rights, claims a progressive stance. This analysis examines their articles to assess whether they reflect this perspective, examining their use of emotionally loaded language, framing of issues, and the factual accuracy of their reporting.
The article
[“How ID Requirements Harm Marginalized Communities and Their Right to Vote”](https://www.democracydocket.com/analysis/how-id-requirements-harm-marginalized-communities-and-their-right-to-vote/) exemplifies Democracy Docket’s progressive bias and criticizes voting ID laws for their complexity and negative impact on marginalized communities. It uses emotionally loaded language, describing the ID acquisition process as “long, winding and confusing.” It cites sources like the Movement Advancement Project, [Bloomberg](https://mediabiasfactcheck.com/bloomberg/), and the Supreme Court’s Georgia ruling to back its argument.
Another article,
[“Two Arizona Voter Suppression Laws Go to Trial,”](https://www.democracydocket.com/analysis/two-arizona-voter-suppression-laws-go-to-trial/) characterizes the Arizona laws as “voter suppression” laws, indicating a bias from the outset. The language used is emotionally loaded, with references to the laws putting “thousands of eligible voters’ registrations at risk” and describing Republican actions as attempts to “circumvent the Supreme Court’s ruling.” Using terms like “anti-voting scheme” and “radical legal theory” further emphasizes a critical, oppositional stance.
The article supports its arguments with references to specific legal challenges,
[court rulings](https://www.democracydocket.com/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/3592023-4-21-reply-iso-speaker-of-the-house-ben-toma-and-senate-presenents-mti.pdf), and legislative actions, indicating a foundation in actual events and legal proceedings. It cites specific bills (HB 2492 and 2243), lawsuits, and sources like eac.gov, azsos.gov.,and [PolitiFact,](https://mediabiasfactcheck.com/politifact/) providing a factual basis for its claims.
In summary, while Democracy Docket exhibits a clear progressive bias in its language and framing, it substantiates its positions with factual information, legal references, and reports, demonstrating a balance between advocacy and factual journalism.
